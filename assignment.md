## The Assignment

Choose one consumer product that you use regularly. For example: Uber · Spotify · Airbnb · Swiggy · Zomato · WhatsApp · YouTube or any consumer application of your choice.

India's Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act, 2023 is India's law governing how organisations collect, use, store, and share people's personal data. Broadly, it emphasises **informed consent**, using data only for the purpose it was collected, giving users rights over their data (such as access, correction, and erasure), and stronger protection for children's data (including verifiable parental consent).

You're encouraged to research the Act and its current requirements yourself as part of this exercise.

**Your task:** as the Product Manager who owns data and privacy for your chosen product, design how the product should handle users' personal data and consent so that it aligns with the DPDP Act — while keeping the experience smooth and not driving users away.

You may tackle the whole data/consent experience, or focus on one high-impact area (for example: the consent experience, children's data protection, or how users exercise their data rights). Take ownership of the complete product-thinking process — from identifying the problem to measuring impact after launch.

## Deliverable 1 — Product Thinking Deck

**Recommended length:** 7–10 slides.

The areas below are what your deck should cover; feel free to combine related ones onto a single slide where it reads naturally (for example, Metrics + Success Criteria).

### 1. Problem Statement

- Which product, and which part of its data/consent handling are you focusing on?
- Why does this problem matter (user trust, legal risk, children's data, etc.)?
- What assumptions have you made?

### 2. User

- Target persona (the person whose data is at stake — e.g., a user, or a parent of a child user)
- User context
- Pain points around privacy and consent today
- Why this problem matters for this user group

### 3. Current Experience

- How the product currently collects, uses, and shares personal data, and how it asks for consent (your understanding / stated assumptions)
- Where it falls short of DPDP expectations
- Key friction or risk points

### 4. Proposed Solution

- Your proposed approach to bring the product in line with the DPDP Act (e.g., consent, data minimisation, user rights, children's data handling)
- How it addresses both the user problem and the compliance need
- Why you chose this solution over the alternatives

### 5. Metrics

Define how you would measure success. Include:

- One **North Star Metric** (for example, the share of active users with valid, current consent — you define what's right)
- 2–3 supporting metrics
- 1–2 guardrail metrics (if applicable)

### 6. Success Criteria

- How will you know your solution worked?
- Include measurable goals wherever possible.

### 7. Diagnostic Thinking

Imagine that three weeks after launch, your North Star Metric drops by 15% — even though no major product release happened. Explain:

- Your hypothesis tree
- Possible reasons for the drop
- What data you would analyse
- How you would identify the root cause
- Your proposed next steps

### 8. Dashboard

Design a simple dashboard to monitor the health of your solution. Include:

- Main KPI
- Trend over time
- Supporting metrics
- At least one segmentation (platform, geography, user cohort, device, age group, etc.)

You may use mock data.

### 9. Rollout Plan

- How you would launch
- How you would validate it
- Your experiment / A/B testing approach, if applicable (note: some compliance elements may not be safely A/B-testable — tell us how you'd handle that)

### 10. Risks & Trade-offs

- Engineering complexity
- Business impact (e.g., added consent steps affecting conversion)
- User impact
- Edge cases (e.g., users who decline consent, child users, data-deletion requests)
- Trade-offs you accepted
